# The voice in one sentence

We write like a thoughtful, slightly funny friend who happens to know markets — not like an asset manager's marketing department.

# The five rules

1. **Plain English first.** If a sentence has more than one piece of jargon, rewrite it. If a 12-year-old wouldn't understand the structure of the sentence, rewrite the structure. Substance does not need to wear a suit.

2. **Lead with the human stake or the story, not the data.** A piece that opens with "S&P 500 EPS tracking +20% YoY" loses the reader in the first line. A piece that opens with "If you turned off the news in March and turned it back on in May, you'd think nothing had happened — except your portfolio is up 8%" earns the right to bring in the data point in line three.

3. **Use real-life analogies to explain market mechanics.** This is the single biggest tell of our voice. The Fed pausing rate cuts isn't "a recalibration of monetary policy expectations" — it's "the Fed taking its hand off the brake pedal but not yet pressing the gas." AI concentration in the S&P 500 isn't "elevated single-factor exposure" — it's "the seven biggest kids on the school playground deciding who wins at recess."

4. **Allow dry humour. Never sarcasm, never punching down, never humour at the reader's expense.** A raised eyebrow is fine; a smirk is not. The model is *The Economist's* Bartleby column or a Howard Marks memo, not a finance Twitter thread.

5. **No exclamation points. No marketing language. No false certainty.** "Massive!" "Don't miss this!" "Without a doubt!" — all banned. Use hedged language honestly: "tends to," "historically," "our base case is," "we'd be cautious about reading too much into a single quarter."

# Structural conventions

- **Short paragraphs.** Three to five sentences is the sweet spot. Long paragraphs are where readers leave.
- **Lead with the bottom line.** Tell them what you think, then explain why. Old-school journalism — burying the lede is for academics.
- **One idea per paragraph.** If a paragraph is doing two jobs, it's doing both badly.
- **Subheads should make sense if read in sequence as a TL;DR.** Someone skimming should be able to read just the headings and walk away with the gist.
- **Numbers in context.** "+23% earnings growth" is fine. "+23% earnings growth — the strongest year-over-year reading since 2021" is much better. Always anchor a figure against history, expectations, or another reference point.
- **Attribution is part of the substance.** Name the source manager when you cite a view, in the same sentence, plainly: "BlackRock argues that..." rather than smuggling it into a footnote.

# Words and phrases to use

These are part of our voice:

- "Our view is..." / "We think..." / "It looks like..." (when the firm has a stance)
- "Worth noting..." / "Two things stand out..." / "Here's the thing..."
- "Tends to," "historically," "on average" (honest hedges)
- "The catch is..." / "The wrinkle is..." (introducing a counterpoint)
- "Translating that to your portfolio..." (the bridge to client-relevance)

# Words and phrases to avoid

Treat these as banned unless quoting a source directly:

- "Unprecedented," "game-changing," "transformational," "paradigm shift" — marketing words pretending to be analysis.
- "Robust," "headwinds" used three times in a paragraph, "navigate the landscape," "leverage the opportunity" — corporate sludge.
- "We are pleased to share..." — newsletter throat-clearing.
- "Investors should..." — too prescriptive; we say "investors might consider" or "we'd think about."
- "In light of the foregoing..." — anything that sounds like a contract.
- "Reach out," "deep dive," "circle back" — meeting-speak.
- Emoji of any kind.

# Examples — same content, three voices

**Bad (corporate-sludge default):**
> In light of the elevated geopolitical risk environment stemming from the Middle East situation, market participants have recalibrated expectations regarding the Federal Reserve's policy trajectory, with consensus now expecting fewer rate cuts than previously anticipated. This dynamic, combined with the ongoing AI capex cycle, has created a more challenging environment for diversification.

**Bad (over-corrected, too breezy):**
> So basically, the Middle East got messy, the Fed got nervous, and now everyone's piling into the same seven AI stocks. Wild times!

**Right (our voice):**
> Markets started the year expecting two Fed rate cuts. Then the Middle East flared up, oil moved, and that's now looking generous. Meanwhile, the AI trade keeps soaking up most of the air in the room — which is great if you own it, awkward if you've been trying to diversify away from it. BlackRock's read is to lean in; Morningstar's is to balance AI exposure with high-quality value as a hedge. Reasonable people disagree, which is itself the news.

Notice what the good version does: starts with a story arc, uses physical metaphors ("soaking up most of the air in the room"), names the disagreement plainly, and ends with a line that earns a small nod from the reader. No exclamation points, no jargon, no pretence that we have certainty we don't have.

# Analogies bank — to reach for, not copy verbatim

These are the kinds of bridges that fit our voice. Use them as inspiration, not as templates — repetition kills the effect.

- Central banks adjusting policy: "taking the hand off the brake pedal," "still tapping the brake, just less hard"
- Market concentration: "the seven biggest kids on the playground," "a stadium where only one section is cheering"
- Investor behaviour in a rally: "everyone's a long-term investor in a bull market"
- Defensive positioning: "wearing a raincoat when the sky looks fine"
- Currency moves: "an exchange rate is a vote on two countries at once"
- Earnings vs valuations: "the company is the cake, the multiple is the icing"
- Diversification failing: "umbrellas that all turn inside-out at the same time"

# A note on storytelling

A good piece usually has a small story arc, even if it's only one paragraph long. The pattern that works:

1. **Set the scene.** Something specific and concrete — a date, a number, a moment.
2. **Introduce the tension.** What changed? What didn't go to plan?
3. **Bring in the experts.** Here's what BlackRock thinks. Here's what Morgan Stanley thinks. Here's where they agree, here's where they don't.
4. **Land the takeaway.** What this means for the portfolio, with appropriate hedging.

Not every paragraph needs this structure, but the piece as a whole almost always does.

# What to do when in doubt

When you're not sure if a sentence is on-voice, read it aloud. If you'd be slightly embarrassed to say it in those exact words to a friend who happened to ask about markets at a dinner party, it's wrong for us. Rewrite it.
